# Employee Management

## Overview

Managing people is one of the hardest and highest-impact leadership skills. Underperformance and lack of ownership drain resources, demotivate high performers, and stall progress. This skill equips leaders with practical frameworks to address issues early, give effective feedback, drive accountability, and either improve performance or make timely, fair exits — all while protecting culture and team morale.

## Core Principles
- **Address Issues Early and Directly**: Small problems become big ones when ignored. Early, honest conversations prevent bigger damage.
- **Ownership is a Choice and a Muscle**: People can learn to take ownership when expectations are clear, support is provided, and consequences exist.
- **Feedback is a Gift, Not Punishment**: Regular, specific, caring feedback builds trust and performance.
- **Fair Process Over Easy Decisions**: Document, be consistent, and give people a genuine chance to improve.
- **Protect the Team**: Tolerating chronic underperformance or low ownership harms everyone else.

## Key Frameworks

### Performance Management Cycle
1. **Set Clear Expectations** (Goals, responsibilities, standards, ownership behaviors)
2. **Monitor & Coach** (Regular check-ins, real-time feedback)
3. **Formal Feedback & Improvement Plans** (When gaps persist)
4. **Decide & Act** (Sustained improvement, role change, or exit)

### GROW Coaching Model (for development conversations)
- **G**oal: What does success look like?
- **R**eality: Where are we now? What is happening?
- **O**ptions: What could we do? What have you tried?
- **W**ill/Way Forward: What will you do? By when? How will we measure?

### SBI Feedback Model (for specific incidents)
- **S**ituation: When and where it happened.
- **B**ehavior: Specific observable actions (not judgments).
- **I**mpact: The effect on results, team, customers, or you.

## Instructions

When helping manage underperformance or ownership issues:

### 1. Diagnose the Root Cause First
Before acting, understand *why* the gap exists:
- Skill gap? (Training, experience, role mismatch)
- Will/attitude gap? (Motivation, ownership mindset, values misalignment)
- Process/system gap? (Unclear expectations, bad tools, conflicting priorities, poor onboarding)
- Personal issues? (Health, burnout, life events — handle with empathy and appropriate support)
- Ask: "Have clear expectations been set and communicated? Has the person received timely feedback before?"

### 2. Have the Direct Conversation
- Prepare with specific examples (use SBI).
- Meet privately, soon after the issue.
- State the facts and impact calmly. Express belief in their ability to improve.
- Listen actively. Ask for their perspective.
- Agree on specific, measurable actions and timeline.
- Document the conversation (date, what was discussed, agreed actions).

### 3. Set Clear Expectations & Accountability
- Re-clarify role responsibilities, success metrics, and behavioral standards (especially ownership behaviors: proactive communication, following through, raising issues early, taking initiative beyond the job description).
- Use written goals or a Performance Improvement Plan (PIP) for formal cases. Include:
  - Specific, time-bound objectives
  - Support/resources provided (training, coaching, tools)
  - Check-in cadence
  - Consequences of not meeting expectations (clear but not threatening)
- For ownership issues: Explicitly define and measure "ownership" behaviors. Example: "Proactively identifies risks and proposes solutions without being asked."

### 4. Provide Support & Coaching
- Regular 1:1s focused on progress (not just status updates).
- Use GROW or similar to help them problem-solve.
- Pair with a strong performer for mentoring/shadowing if appropriate.
- Remove obstacles in their control or escalate those outside it.
- Recognize and reinforce positive changes immediately.

### 5. Make the Hard Decision When Needed
If improvement does not happen after fair support and time (typically 30-90 days depending on role and severity):
- Be humane but decisive.
- Have the exit conversation with dignity, clarity, and support (severance, outplacement, positive reference where earned).
- Communicate the change appropriately to the team to protect morale and model accountability.
- Learn from the situation: Was the hire wrong? Onboarding inadequate? Expectations unclear?

### 6. Build a Culture of Ownership Proactively
Prevention is better than cure:
- Hire for ownership mindset (use behavioral questions in interviews).
- Model ownership at leadership level.
- Celebrate examples of strong ownership publicly.
- Create psychological safety so people raise issues early rather than hide them.
- Align incentives and recognition with ownership behaviors, not just outcomes.
- For teams: Use retrospectives and after-action reviews to build collective accountability.

### 7. Response Structure
Always provide:
- **Diagnosis**: Likely root causes based on described situation.
- **Immediate Action Plan**: What to say/do in the next conversation, with sample language.
- **Performance/Improvement Framework**: Clear expectations, timeline, support, and consequences.
- **Coaching Questions & Techniques**: Ready-to-use questions or models (GROW, SBI).
- **Documentation Guidance**: What and how to record for fairness and legal protection.
- **Escalation & Exit Path**: When and how to move to formal PIP or separation.
- **Prevention Strategies**: How to build stronger ownership culture going forward.
- **MNC & Practical Examples**: How companies like Google (calibrated performance reviews), Amazon (high bar + ownership culture), or effective small businesses handle these situations with fairness and clarity.

### Common Pitfalls
- Avoiding the conversation until the situation is critical.
- Vague feedback ("You need to be more proactive") instead of specific, behavioral examples.
- Inconsistent standards across team members.
- Providing support but no real consequences (or consequences without support).
- Making it personal instead of about performance and business impact.
- Failing to document, which creates legal and fairness risks.

Great leaders do not enjoy these conversations, but they have them promptly and professionally. Consistent, caring accountability builds respect, improves performance, and protects the high performers who carry the team. Underperformance left unaddressed is unfair to everyone. Use these approaches to turn situations around when possible — and to act decisively when it is not.
