# Working Capital Management

## Overview

Working capital (current assets minus current liabilities) is the lifeblood that keeps operations running. Companies with strong working capital management survive economic shocks, supply chain disruptions, and demand swings; those with poor management often face liquidity crises and bankruptcy even with strong products or sales. This skill provides deep, practical strategies drawn from resilient companies versus cautionary tales.

## Core Principles
- **Cash Conversion Cycle (CCC) is King**: Shorten the time between paying for inputs and collecting cash from customers.
- **Quality Over Quantity**: Not all working capital is equal. Excess inventory or slow receivables destroy value.
- **Proactive vs Reactive**: Build buffers and flexibility before crises hit. Crisis management starts in good times.
- **Trade-offs are Real**: Faster growth often requires more working capital. Balance ambition with liquidity discipline.
- **Visibility & Forecasting**: You cannot manage what you do not measure in real time.

## Key Formula
\[
Cash\ Conversion\ Cycle\ (days) = DIO + DSO - DPO
\]
Where:
- DIO = Days Inventory Outstanding = (Average Inventory / COGS) × 365
- DSO = Days Sales Outstanding = (Accounts Receivable / Revenue) × 365
- DPO = Days Payable Outstanding = (Accounts Payable / COGS) × 365

Goal: Shorten CCC without damaging operations or relationships.

## Instructions

When helping with working capital:

### 1. Diagnose Current State
- Calculate and trend the CCC and its components over 12+ months.
- Break down by business unit, product line, or customer segment.
- Identify the biggest lever: Inventory? Receivables? Payables?
- Benchmark against industry peers and best-in-class companies.

### 2. Inventory Management (Often the Largest Opportunity)
- Implement just-in-time or demand-driven replenishment where possible (inspired by Toyota Production System).
- Use ABC analysis: Focus tight control on high-value "A" items.
- Reduce safety stock through better forecasting, supplier collaboration, or postponement strategies.
- Liquidate slow-moving or obsolete inventory aggressively (even at a loss) to free cash.
- For manufacturers/retail: Adopt vendor-managed inventory or consignment where suppliers hold stock until needed.
- Pitfall to avoid: Overstocking "just in case" during uncertainty — it often leads to bigger write-downs later.

### 3. Receivables Optimization
- Tighten credit policies for new or risky customers. Use credit scoring or insurance.
- Offer early payment discounts (e.g., 2/10 net 30) selectively.
- Automate collections with dunning sequences and dedicated AR team focus.
- For project-based: Milestone billing and upfront deposits.
- Segment customers by payment behavior and prioritize high-value, slow-paying accounts.

### 4. Payables Strategy
- Negotiate longer payment terms with suppliers (without jeopardizing supply or relationships).
- Prioritize strategic suppliers for early payment if it unlocks discounts or priority allocation.
- Use supply chain finance or dynamic discounting programs when available.
- Avoid stretching payables to the point of damaging supplier health or delivery reliability.

### 5. Crisis Survival Strategies (What Separates Survivors from Bankruptcies)
- **Survivors** (examples: many during 2008, 2020, or supply chain crises):
  - Maintained strong cash reserves and undrawn credit lines before the crisis.
  - Aggressively managed inventory down and accelerated collections early.
  - Renegotiated terms with suppliers and landlords proactively.
  - Focused on core profitable customers and shed marginal ones.
  - Preserved key talent and supplier relationships even while cutting elsewhere.
- **Failures** often:
  - Ran with minimal cash buffer and high leverage.
  - Continued aggressive inventory builds or long payment cycles.
  - Delayed difficult decisions until liquidity was gone.
  - Lost supplier or customer trust through last-minute demands.

**Action in crisis**: Immediate 13-week cash forecast, prioritize collections, defer all non-essential capex and hiring, negotiate payment plans, explore asset-backed lending or sale-leaseback if needed.

### 6. Building Resilient Working Capital Systems
- Implement real-time dashboards for inventory, AR, AP aging.
- Set target ranges for DIO, DSO, DPO, and CCC by business type.
- Integrate working capital targets into performance management and incentives.
- Review working capital as part of monthly/quarterly business reviews alongside P&L.
- For scaling companies: Model working capital needs as part of growth planning (e.g., "every $1M revenue growth requires $X additional working capital").

### 7. Response Structure
Deliver:
- **Working Capital Health Check**: Current CCC, trends, component breakdown, and comparison to benchmarks.
- **Biggest Opportunities**: Ranked list of levers with estimated cash impact and effort.
- **Action Plan**: 30/60/90-day initiatives with owners.
- **Crisis Playbook** (if relevant): Immediate liquidity preservation steps.
- **MNC & Case Examples**: Toyota (lean inventory & JIT), Walmart (efficient supply chain & payables power), Amazon (negative CCC in many categories through scale and efficiency), vs. companies that failed due to inventory bloat or receivables problems.
- **Ongoing Monitoring**: Recommended KPIs and review cadence.

### Common Pitfalls
- Treating working capital as a finance-only issue instead of cross-functional (sales, operations, procurement).
- Growing revenue without modeling the working capital investment required.
- Sacrificing supplier or customer relationships for short-term DPO/DSO wins.
- Ignoring seasonality or business model differences (e.g., project vs. subscription vs. retail).
- Building large buffers without addressing root process inefficiencies.

Mastering working capital turns volatility into competitive advantage. Companies that manage it well generate cash from operations even in tough times, funding growth internally and surviving when others cannot. This is a core discipline for long-term business resilience.
