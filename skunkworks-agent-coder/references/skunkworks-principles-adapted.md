# Skunkworks Principles — Adapted for Modern Agentic Development

## Original Lockheed Skunkworks Rules (Kelly Johnson’s 14 Rules — highly relevant excerpts)
- The Skunk Works manager must be delegated practically complete control of his program in all aspects.
- Strong but small project offices must be provided both by the military and industry.
- A very simple drawing and drawing release system with great flexibility for making changes must be provided.
- There must be a minimum number of reports required, but important work must be recorded thoroughly.
- The contractor must be delegated the authority to test his final product in flight.
- The specifications applying to the hardware must be agreed to in advance of contracting.
- Funding must be timely.
- There must be mutual trust between the military project organization and the contractor.
- Access by outsiders to the project and its personnel must be strictly controlled.

## Modern Agentic Translation
- Give the small agent team real autonomy over architecture, tools, and iteration speed.
- Keep the "project office" (any oversight) tiny and high-signal.
- Favor working code and living docs over heavy documentation and change control boards.
- Instrument evaluation and observability from day one — this is your "flight test data".
- Protect the team from endless meetings, status reports, and scope creep from outside.
- Build trust through rapid, transparent results rather than process theater.

## Key Differences from Traditional Agile / Scrum for Agent Work
Traditional agile often adds too much ceremony for the speed at which agent capabilities can be prototyped and invalidated.
Skunkworks is closer to "extreme programming + extreme ownership + extreme evaluation" than to scaled agile frameworks.

## When Skunkworks Evolves into Something Else
Most successful skunkworks agent projects eventually transition:
- High-value agents → dedicated product/engineering team with more process.
- Exploratory or one-off agents → maintained lightly or deprecated.
- Platform capabilities discovered during skunkworks → fed into core platform/ontology work.

Plan the transition explicitly rather than letting the team drift or burn out.

These principles have produced some of the fastest and most innovative engineering outcomes in history when applied with discipline and protected by leadership.
