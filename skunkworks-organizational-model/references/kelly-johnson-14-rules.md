# Kelly Johnson's 14 Rules of Skunk Works (Condensed)

These rules, developed by Clarence "Kelly" Johnson, are the foundation of the Skunk Works model. They remain highly relevant for any organization seeking extreme speed and innovation.

1. **The Skunk Works Manager must have practically complete control** of the program in all aspects (technical, financial, personnel).

2. **Strong but small project offices** must be provided by both the customer and the contractor.

3. **The number of people having any connection with the project must be restricted** in an almost vicious manner. Use a small number of good people (10-25% of normal team size).

4. **A very simple drawing and drawing release system** with great flexibility for making changes must be provided.

5. **There must be a minimum number of reports required**, but important work must be recorded thoroughly.

6. **There must be a monthly cost review** covering not only what has been spent and committed but also projected costs to the conclusion of the program.

7. **The contractor must be delegated the authority to test his final product in flight**. (Design engineers should participate in testing.)

8. **The specifications applying to the hardware must be agreed to well in advance** of contracting. (Avoid constant requirement changes.)

9. **The Skunk Works practice of having a specification section** stating clearly which important military specification items will not knowingly be complied with and reasons therefore is highly recommended.

10. **The specification for hardware should be agreed upon** by the government and the contractor before the contract is signed.

11. **Funding should be timely** so that the contractor does not have to keep running to the bank to support his program.

12. **There must be mutual trust between the military project organization and the contractor**, with very close cooperation and liaison on a day-to-day basis.

13. **Access by outsiders to the project and its personnel** must be strictly controlled by appropriate security measures.

14. **Because only a few people will be used in engineering and most other areas**, ways must be provided to reward good performance by pay, not simply by promoting to management positions.

These rules emphasize **empowerment, minimalism, trust, speed, and focus on technical contribution over bureaucracy**.

Modern adaptations for AI/agent development, software, or hybrid systems should preserve the spirit: radical autonomy for a small elite team, minimal process, direct senior sponsorship, and rewards based on results rather than organizational size.
